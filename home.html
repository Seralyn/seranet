This is example text that should show up when hovering over an image of the reflector telescope. In a reflector, light enters the telescope at the end opposite the primary mirror. The mirror is a concave mirror. Similar to a convex lens, a concave mirror converges the light at the secondary mirror. The rays of light converge at the focal point. At this point they again begin to diverge. The convex lens in the eyepiece takes the converging light and straightens it back out. As with the refractor, the image is still upside down. It appears as a virtual image beyond the telescope in the direction the person is looking.

Well, whilst a reflector telescope uses mirrors and a refractor telescope uses lenses, a catadioptric telescope actually uses both. When the light comes through the telescope, it first passes through a collective lens. This lens is there to correct the image and helps to reduce any kind of aberration that may occur and distort the final image from reaching your eyes. The light then passes through to the primary mirrors, which are curved. This reflection is then cast onto the secondary mirror, which then comes through to the eyepiece.
