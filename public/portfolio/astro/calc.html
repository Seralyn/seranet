Formulas and Calculators

Calculate Magnification Factor for Observing

Although it is not neccessary to know what magnification factor you are viewing the heavens with, it can often be handy in calculating other things, such as maximum magnification, or just for general knowledge. Below is a formula on how to calculate it, as well as a handy calculator tool if you're feeling lazy or are in a hurry. Or perhaps, like me, you could do with less math in your life.


Telescope focal length / Eyepiece focal length = Magnification Factor



If using a Barlow Lens or Focal Extender, add that X factor to the end result of the previous calculation or multiply the original focal length of the telescope beforehand. Both result in the same number.

Example: 1500mm focal length telescope / 25mm eyepiece = 60X x Barlow Mag. Factor (if used directly below the eyepice) of 2x = 120x

Effective Maximum Magnification for Observing

Although actual maximum magnification can sometimes be higher, what we'll concern ourselves with on this section is maximum "effective" magnification. Anything higher is inviting frustration in my opinion. Despite what your telescope and eyepieces may be capable of, the atmospheric conditions of the sky most often limit this to no more than 250-300x. To calculate the maximum magnification factor of your telescope and currently used eyepiece, use the formula below as a rule of thumb.

Aperture of Telescope in inches X 50 = Maximum Effective Magnification

Example: 5 inch telescope X 50 = 250X max effective magnification

Aperture of Telescope in millimeters X 2 = Maximum Effective Magnification

Example: 127mm telescope X 2 = 252X max effective magnification

Use this information with the calculator above to determine how far you should expect to be able to push your observation magnification, generally speaking.

Pixel Ratio - Sampling

Pixel Ratio is all about matching the ratio of the pixels of your camera to the resolution of your optical train. Pixel Ratios below a certain threshold are considered to be "undersampled" while pixel ratios above that threshold are considered to be "oversampled."
